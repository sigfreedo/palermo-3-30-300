The presence of nature in cities improves health and livability.

According to the European Environment Agency, contact with urban greenery — parks, gardens, green spaces for rest and physical activity — reduces stress, improves physical and mental well-being and mitigates urban temperatures by reducing the heat island effect.

But not everyone in Europe has the same access to green spaces.

Palermo 3-30-300 is a small independent research project created to spread awareness about urban green space availability, address by address, based on an international rule that helps us design more livable, cool and sustainable cities.

🌳 La regola del 3+30+300 per città più verdi

This principle, developed by Cecil Konijnendijk, explains the importance of bringing nature into neighborhoods, streets and right in front of our homes, to reap its numerous benefits. It provides clear guiding criteria for the minimum provision of trees in our urban communities, establishing the following three minimum requirements:

  • 3 trees maturi visibili da ogni abitazione
  • 30% tree canopy coverage in every neighborhood
  • 300 meters maximum distance from the nearest public green space for everyone

This study inverts the order, starting from the 300-meter criterion: the metric that best measures equity in public green access for every citizen.

🎯 The accessibility standard

Everyone should have access to a high-quality, publicly accessible green space, no more than 300 meters away on foot or by bicycle, for recreational activities and promoting healthier lifestyles.

💚 Why it matters

Proximity to green spaces improves physical health by encouraging daily movement and promotes mental well-being by reducing stress and anxiety. Green spaces are also places for community gathering and socializing and help mitigate urban heat islands.

⏱️ What 300 meters means

  • About 4 minutes walking
  • An accessible daily walk
  • Distance walkable even by children and elderly

Fonti: Nature-Based Solutions Institute, The Threes Rule, Yggdrasil

🌳 The 30% rule

Stabilisce che almeno il 30% della superficie urbana dovrebbe essere coperta da chiome arboree (tree canopy) per garantire benefici ambientali e di salute pubblica excellentli. Uno studio della Commissione Europea su 93 città europee ha dimostrato che raggiungere questo standard ridurrebbe la temperatura average di 0,4°C, con picchi fino a 5,9°C in alcune zone.

🍃 Cosa significa tree canopy?

The tree canopy is the upper layer formed by tree crowns — the collection of branches, leaves and foliage that project vertically to the ground. It is measured through satellite imagery and represents the area actually shaded by tree vegetation. This coverage works as a natural air conditioner: a single mature tree can produce the cooling effect of ten residential air conditioners running 20 hours a day.

💚 Why it matters?

Cities with adequate tree canopy coverage register temperatures up to 8°C lower compared to areas without trees, drastically reducing the "heat island" effect. Trees filter atmospheric pollutants like PM2.5 and PM10, improving the air quality we breathe daily. 30% tree canopy coverage could prevent 6,700 premature deaths every summer in European cities.

On the economic front, homes surrounded by trees sell for 5% to 15% more than those without vegetation, reaching up to 25% if the entire street is tree-lined. Trees reduce energy costs by 20-50% for summer cooling and 10-15% for winter heating. Finally, by absorbing large amounts of rainwater, they prevent flooding and reduce the load on urban drainage infrastructure.

Fonti: Canopy, Citygreen, European Commission

Tree canopy coverage a Palermo

From the tree canopy coverage perspective, Palermo falls well below the recommended 30% standard. Exemplary cities like Oslo (Norvegia, 45%), Torino (Italia, 34%), e Vienna (Austria, 50%) reach and widely exceed this threshold, ensuring their citizens the environmental and health benefits of adequate tree canopy coverage.

The data on tree canopy coverage comes from the Copernicus Tree Cover Density 2023 and were cross-referenced with census sections ISTAT 2021 to calculate the average coverage per neighborhood. The estimate of trees needed is based on mature trees with an average canopy of 35 m².

🌳 La regola del 3

Stabilisce che tutti dovrebbero poter vedere almeno tre trees adulti dalla propria casa, dal posto di lavoro e dalla scuola. Questo principio nasce dall'evidenza scientifica che la semplice visibilità del verde dalla propria finestra produce benefici significativi per il benessere psicologico, anche senza accesso fisico diretto alla natura.

🧠 Benefici per la salute mentale

A 2024 study demonstrated that people who can see at least three trees from their windows report significantly higher levels of connection with nature and mental well-being compared to those who do not have this possibility. The view of trees reduces stress and anxiety, improves mood and promotes psychophysical recovery.

I pazienti con vista sugli trees dalle finestre ospedaliere guariscono più velocemente, richiedono meno farmaci e hanno meno complicazioni rispetto a quelli con vista sugli edifici.

📚 Benefici per la concentrazione

A series of studies conducted in the United States demonstrated that students in schools with greater tree canopy coverage achieve better academic results, especially when trees are visible from classroom and cafeteria windows. The view of greenery promotes concentration and reduces mental fatigue through what is called attention restoration, the natural recovery of attention capacity.

💼 Benefici sul lavoro

Le persone che possono vedere la natura mentre lavorano si prendono meno giorni di malattia (-23%, circa 11 ore all'anno) e riportano una maggiore soddisfazione lavorativa. Even breaks of just 3-4 minutes near trees produce measurable reductions in stress levels.

Fonti: Zhang et al. (2024), El País, Matsuoka (2010), Kweon et al. (2014), Wu et al. (2018)
